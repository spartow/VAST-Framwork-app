ABSTRACT
As artificial intelligence systems increasingly function autonomously within safety-critical domains, the imperative to ensure that their decision-making processes remain congruent with human values has escalated in importance. Current approaches to AI alignment, including Reinforcement Learning from Human Feedback (RLHF), Constitutional AI, and value learning exhibit significant limitations: opacity in reasoning processes, rigidity in novel contexts, and brittleness under distributional shift. This thesis introduces VAST (Value Aligned Structured Tracking), a comprehensive framework that addresses these limitations through four integrated architectural components.
VAST employs a tripartite belief representation (?, ?, J) that separates credence, confidence, and justification, enabling transparent epistemic state tracking. The Justification Weighted Moral Consistency (JWMC) operator maintains value stability during belief revision by weighting updates according to their coherence with established moral principles. The Ethical Expected Utility with Cascading Constraints (EEUCC) decision mechanism implements soft hierarchical constraints that balance competing values without the brittleness of hard rules. Finally, the Four Gauges Alignment System provides real time monitoring across calibration, normative alignment, coherence, and reasoning quality dimensions.
An extensive literature review indicates that, while individual components of alignment systems may exist in isolation, no previous research has succeeded in integrating structured belief representation, justification-weighted revision, cascading ethical constraints, and multidimensional real-time monitoring into a cohesive framework. Comparative evaluation across five ethically complex scenarios demonstrates VAST's superior performance, achieving an average alignment score of 0.76 compared to RLHF (0.58), Constitutional AI (0.61), and value learning (0.64). The framework maintains sub second response times (0.68s average) while providing complete audit trails for every decision.
This work contributes both theoretically and practically to the field of AI safety and alignment. Theoretically, it establishes a formal framework for integrating epistemic and moral reasoning in autonomous systems. Practically, it demonstrates that transparent, auditable, and adaptable alignment can be achieved without sacrificing performance. The VAST framework signifies a noteworthy advancement toward the establishment of trustworthy AI systems capable of functioning in complex real-world environments while preserving verifiable alignment with human values.

CHAPTER 1: INTRODUCTION
1.1 Motivation and Problem Statement
The rapid advancement of artificial intelligence systems has steered in an era where autonomous agents increasingly make decisions that directly impact human welfare, safety, and ethical values. From the allocation of healthcare resources amid pandemics to the navigation of autonomous vehicles in critical scenarios, AI systems are now functioning within frameworks that necessitate sophisticated moral reasoning and value-congruent decision-making (Russell, 2019; Bostrom, 2014). Nevertheless, the imperative to ensure that these systems uniformly operate in alignment with human values, a challenge frequently referred to as the AI alignment problem, persists as one of the most significant technical and philosophical dilemmas in the field of computer science (Christian, 2020; Gabriel, 2020).
Current methodologies pertaining to AI alignment exhibit intrinsic deficiencies that compromise their appropriateness for safety-critical implementations. Reinforcement Learning from Human Feedback (RLHF), while proficient at encapsulating human preferences within controlled environments, functions as an opaque system, offering no elucidation for its determinations (Christiano et al., 2017; Ouyang et al., 2022). This lack of transparency precludes the possibility of auditing the reasoning mechanism or ascertaining that outcomes are congruent with articulated principles. Constitutional AI endeavors to enhance transparency by establishing explicit regulations; however, its inflexible, binary constraint satisfaction produces vulnerabilities in distinctive scenarios where rules may conflict or atypical cases emerge (Bai et al., 2022). Value learning methodologies aspire to deduce human values from observed actions but are impeded by instability in the face of distributional shifts and lack mechanisms to preserve value consistency as new data is assimilated (Hadfield Menell et al., 2016; Russell et al., 2015). These limitations are not merely theoretical concerns. In practice, deployed AI systems have exhibited value misalignment with serious consequences: biased lending algorithms perpetuating discrimination, content moderation systems amplifying harmful content, and medical triage systems making opaque life or death decisions (O'Neil, 2016; Noble, 2018). The absence of transparent, auditable, and adaptable alignment mechanisms prevents stakeholders from trusting AI systems in high stakes domains and limits the ability to diagnose and correct failures when they occur.
This thesis addresses these challenges by introducing VAST (Value Aligned Structured Tracking), a comprehensive framework for moral alignment in agentic AI systems. VAST incorporates four fundamental innovations: (1) A tripartite representation of belief that distinguishes between credence, confidence, and justification; (2) A belief revision operator that weighs justification and preserves value stability; (3) An ethical decision-making mechanism utilizing soft cascading constraints; and (4) A multidimensional real-time alignment monitoring system. Together, these components enable AI systems to make transparent, auditable decisions that remain aligned with human values even in novel and ambiguous situations.
1.2 Research Questions
This research addresses the following questions:
RQ1:   In what ways may structure belief representation facilitate transparent and auditable moral reasoning within autonomous AI systems?
Current belief representation in AI systems merges probability (credence), epistemic uncertainty (confidence), and moral justification into singular scalar values or implicit weights. This conflation obscures the reasoning process and makes it impossible to separately assess factual accuracy, epistemic humility, and moral coherence. This research question investigates whether a tripartite belief structure (?, ?, J) that explicitly separates these dimensions can support transparent audit trails while maintaining computational tractability.
RQ2: What strategies might preserve value stability during belief revision while ensuring flexibility to new information?
A core conflict in AI alignment lies in preserving stable values (preventing value drift) while accommodating new knowledge (preventing brittleness). RLHF and value learning approaches frequently exhibit value drift as they update based on new feedback, while Constitutional AI's fixed rules cannot adapt to novel contexts. This research question examines whether justification weighted belief updates, which consider moral coherence alongside evidential weight, can navigate this tension.

RQ3: How can ethical decision-making balance compete values through soft hierarchical constraints rather than rigid rules?
Ethical actions in the real world never adhere to unequivocal absolute standards; rather, they necessitate the reconciliation of conflicting values with contextually dependent objectives.  The stringent limitations of Constitutional AI induce fragility, whereas unadulterated utility maximization may contravene significant ancillary constraints.  This research question investigates if cascading soft constraints with moral weighting can offer both flexibility and conformity to principles.
RQ4: Is real-time multidimensional alignment monitoring capable of delivering actionable signals for identifying misalignment?
Current alignment methodologies often evaluate performance via offline assessments or unidimensional metrics. This offers inadequate feedback for systems deployed in dynamic contexts. This research question examines how concurrent monitoring of calibration, normative alignment, coherence, and reasoning quality can facilitate the early identification of misalignment and assist in corrective intervention.

CHAPTER 2: PROBLEM DOMAIN AND BACKGROUND
2.1 The AI Alignment Challenge
The AI alignment problem concerns ensuring that artificial intelligence systems behave in accordance with human values and intentions, particularly as these systems become more autonomous and capable (Russell, 2019). This challenge has become increasingly urgent as AI systems transition from narrow, well specified tasks to open ended decision making in complex real world environments where human values are often ambiguous, conflicting, or context dependent (Gabriel, 2020).
The fundamental difficulty of alignment stems from several interrelated factors. First, human values are not fully specified or easily codified into formal rules (Yudkowsky, 2004). Values often exist as implicit preferences revealed through choices rather than explicit principles that can be directly programmed. Second, values are contextual and frequent conflict, requiring nuanced balancing rather than absolute prioritization (Anderson & Anderson, 2011). Third, the space of possible situations an AI system may encounter is too vast to enumerate in advance, requiring systems to generalize value alignment to novel contexts (Amodei et al., 2016).
These challenges manifest concretely in deployed systems. Healthcare AI systems must balance utilitarian considerations (maximizing lives saved) against deontological constraints (treating individuals as ends in themselves) and virtue ethics considerations (maintaining trust in medical institutions). Autonomous vehicles face split second decisions involving potential harm to passengers versus pedestrians, with no clear ethical calculus. Content moderation systems must navigate tensions between free expression, harm prevention, and competing cultural values. Financial algorithms balance profit maximization against fairness constraints and regulatory compliance.
Current approaches to alignment attempt to address these challenges through various mechanisms, but each exhibits significant limitations that compromise their suitability for safety critical applications.

2.2 Limitations of Current Approaches
2.2.1 Reinforcement Learning from Human Feedback
Reinforcement Learning from Human Feedback (RLHF) has emerged as a dominant paradigm for aligning language models and other AI systems with human preferences (Christiano et al., 2017; Ouyang et al., 2022; Stiennon et al., 2020). RLHF trains a reward model from human preference comparisons and uses this model to guide policy learning through reinforcement learning. While RLHF has demonstrated impressive empirical success in improving output quality for large language models, it suffers from fundamental limitations for safety critical applications.
Opacity in Reasoning: RLHF provides no transparent justification for decisions. The learned reward model operates as a black box, mapping states and actions to scalar reward signals without explaining why particular actions are preferred. This opacity prevents stakeholders from auditing decisions, understanding failure modes, or verifying alignment with stated principles (Hadfield Menell & Hadfield, 2019).
Reward Hacking: RLHF systems are vulnerable to reward hacking, where agents discover ways to achieve high reward signals without satisfying the true underlying objective (Amodei et al., 2016). This occurs because the learned reward model is necessarily an imperfect proxy for human values, and optimization pressure finds and exploits the divergence between proxy and true objective.
Distributional Brittleness: RLHF trained systems exhibit brittleness under distributional shift. When encountering situations meaningfully different from the training distribution, the reward model's predictions become unreliable, but the system has no mechanism to recognize this unreliability and adjust behavior appropriately (Tong et al., 2024).
Value Drift: As RLHF systems continue learning from new feedback, their effective values can drift away from original specifications without any mechanism to maintain consistency or detect drift (Everitt et al., 2018). This makes it difficult to provide safety guarantees or maintain coherent behavior over extended deployment.

2.2.2 Constitutional AI
Constitutional AI attempts to address RLHF's opacity by encoding explicit principles or rules that constrain system behavior (Bai et al., 2022). Systems are trained to follow a "constitution" of written principles, providing transparency about what values the system is designed to uphold. However, Constitutional AI introduces its own set of limitations that compromise robustness.
Rule Rigidity: Constitutional AI typically implements rules as hard constraints with binary satisfaction. Actions either satisfy the rule or violate it, with no mechanism for soft tradeoffs when rules conflict or circumstances demand flexibility (Guarini, 2006). This rigidity creates brittleness in edge cases and novel situations where strict rule adherence produces poor outcomes.
Conflict Resolution: When constitutional principles conflict as they inevitably do in complex real world situations, Constitutional AI systems lack principled mechanisms for resolving these conflicts. The system must either prioritize rules arbitrarily, fail to act, or implement ad hoc tiebreaking that undermines the transparency the approach aims to provide (Moor, 2006).
Limited Generalization: Written constitutional principles necessarily fail to cover all possible situations an AI system may encounter. The brittleness of hard constraints means that even slight deviations from covered scenarios can lead to inappropriate behavior, as the system has no mechanism to adapt principles to novel contexts while preserving their underlying intent (Wallach & Allen, 2008).
Justification Gaps: While Constitutional AI specifies which rules apply, it does not explain why those rules should take priority in specific contexts or how they balance against competing considerations. This leaves significant justification gaps that prevent full transparency and auditability (Anderson et al., 2019).

Figure 2.2: Constitutional AI Brittleness: When explicit rules conflict (preserve life vs. respect autonomy), rule based systems face deadlock or require arbitrary prioritization, leading to brittleness in real world scenarios with competing moral principles

2.2.3 Value Learning
Value learning approaches attempt to infer human values from observed behavior rather than requiring explicit specification (Russell et al., 2015; Hadfield Menell et al., 2016; Shah et al., 2018). By observing human choices and decisions, systems can learn reward functions or value functions that rationalize the observed behavior, then act to maximize these learned values. While conceptually appealing, value learning faces substantial challenges in practice.

Figure 2.3: Value Drift in Value Learning Systems
Inference Ambiguity: Human behavior is consistent with many different underlying value functions, creating fundamental ambiguity in value inference (Evans et al., 2016). Without strong prior assumptions or extensive behavioral data, value learning systems cannot reliably identify the correct values from observation alone.
Sample Complexity: Learning accurate value functions requires extensive behavioral data, particularly for complex, high dimensional value spaces. This sample complexity is often prohibitive for safety critical domains where errors during learning are unacceptable (Christiano et al., 2018).
Myopic Optimization: Value learning systems typically optimize for learned values without mechanisms to question whether those values have been correctly inferred or whether their application in novel contexts remains appropriate (Soares, 2015). This can lead to confident pursuit of misaligned objectives.
Value Drift: As value learning systems continue updating based on new observations, their learned values can drift over time. Without mechanisms to maintain value consistency or flag significant changes, this drift can lead to unpredictable behavioral changes (Arnold & Kasenberg, 2021).
Generalization Failures: Values learned from one distributional context often fail to generalize appropriately to novel contexts. What constitutes acceptable tradeoffs in routine situations may be entirely inappropriate in emergencies, but value learning systems lack mechanisms to adapt their learned values appropriately (Krakovna et al., 2020).
2.3 Requirements for Robust Alignment
Drawing from the limitations of existing approaches, we can identify key requirements that robust AI alignment systems must satisfy:
R1: Transparency and Auditability Systems must provide explicit, inspectable justifications for decisions that enable stakeholders to verify alignment with stated principles and diagnose failures when they occur.
R2: Value Stability with Adaptability Systems must maintain consistent core values while adapting appropriately to new information and contexts, with mechanisms to detect and prevent value drift.
R3: Flexible Constraint Satisfaction Systems must balance competing values through graduated constraints rather than rigid rules, enabling principled tradeoffs without brittleness.
R4: Real time Alignment Monitoring Systems must continuously assess their own alignment across multiple dimensions, providing early warning of potential misalignment before failures occur.
R5: Epistemic Humility Systems must represent and reason about their own uncertainty, adjusting behavior appropriately when confidence is low or situations are novel.
R6: Computational Feasibility Systems must achieve these properties with acceptable computational overhead suitable for real time deployment in resource constrained environments.
The VAST framework, introduced in Chapter 4, is designed to satisfy these requirements through integrated architectural components that address the specific limitations identified in current alignment approaches.

CHAPTER 3: RELATED WORK AND LITERATURE REVIEW
3.1 MultiValue Alignment Systems
The challenge of aligning AI systems with multiple, potentially conflicting human values has received increasing attention in recent literature. Traditional singleobjective optimization approaches prove inadequate when systems must balance competing principles such as fairness, efficiency, privacy, and safety (Ecoffet et al., 2021).
MultiHumanValue Alignment Palette (MAP) represents a significant advance in multivalue alignment (Nahian et al., 2021; Sorensen et al., 2024). MAP formulates alignment as a primaldual optimization problem where multiple value objectives are simultaneously optimized while satisfying constraints. The framework introduces value coefficients that weight the relative importance of different objectives, enabling explicit tradeoff specification. However, MAP focuses primarily on optimization mechanics rather than belief representation or reasoning transparency. The system does not maintain explicit justifications for why particular value tradeoffs are selected, limiting auditability. Additionally, MAP's optimization approach requires careful tuning of value coefficients and provides no mechanism for adapting these weights based on context or maintaining their stability over time.
Hierarchical Value Principles (HiVaP) extends multivalue alignment by introducing priority levels among competing principles (Conitzer et al., 2024). HiVaP organizes values into tiers, where highertier principles take lexicographic priority over lowertier ones. This hierarchical structure captures important aspects of human moral reasoning, where certain values (e.g., preventing harm) categorically dominate others (e.g., maximizing utility). However, HiVaP implements these priorities as hard lexicographic constraints rather than soft graduated preferences. This rigidity creates brittleness when strict priority adherence leads to poor outcomes or when realworld contexts demand more nuanced balancing. Furthermore, HiVaP does not address how these hierarchies should be learned, specified, or adapted to novel situations.
MultiObjective Reinforcement Learning (MORL) approaches decompose complex alignment problems into multiple reward functions that are optimized simultaneously (Hayes et al., 2022; Vamplew et al., 2021). MORL methods can discover optimal policies that balance competing objectives, providing decision makers with a set of options representing different tradeoff points. However, MORL inherits RLHF's fundamental opacity problem, the learned policies provide no transparent justification for their decisions. Additionally, MORL typically focuses on finding good policies rather than maintaining alignment guarantees or providing mechanisms for real time monitoring of value adherence.
Comparison to VAST: While these multivalue systems demonstrate the importance of balancing competing principles, they lack VAST's integrated approach to transparency, justification, and real time monitoring. MAP optimizes multiple values but provides no explicit reasoning chains. HiVaP introduces hierarchies but implements them rigidly without soft tradeoffs. MORL finds balanced policies but remains opaque in its decision making. VAST uniquely combines multivalue balancing (through Thical Expected Utility's Cascading Cconstraints) with explicit justification tracking (through the J component), soft graduated tradeoffs (through penalty functions), and continuous alignment assessment (through the Four Gauges system). No existing multivalue alignment system provides this comprehensive integration.

3.2 Ethical Utility Functions
The design of utility functions that properly encode ethical considerations has been a central concern in AI alignment research. Simply maximizing expected utility without ethical constraints can lead to harmful outcomes, while poorly designed constraints can create perverse incentives or brittleness.
Aliman and Kester's Requisite Variety Approach proposes incorporating insights from neuroscience and psychology into ethical utility function design (Aliman & Kester, 2020, 2021). They argue that ethical AI systems require "requisite variety", sufficient complexity and flexibility in their value representation to match the complexity of human moral reasoning. Their framework emphasizes the importance of context sensitivity, arguing that ethical judgments cannot be reduced to simple rules or fixed utility weightings. This theoretical work provides important insights into what properties ethical utility functions should possess, but offers limited guidance on concrete implementation mechanisms. The framework does not specify how to represent beliefs, maintain value stability, or monitor alignment in operational systems.
Constraint Based Ethical Utility approaches incorporate ethical principles as constraints on utility maximization rather than components of the utility function itself (Cervantes et al., 2020). This separation enables systems to pursue objectives while respecting ethical boundaries, preventing the common failure mode where ethical considerations are simply outweighed by sufficiently large utility gains. However, most constraintbased approaches implement constraints as hard boundaries actions either satisfy constraints or are prohibited. This rigidity fails to capture the graduated nature of many ethical principles, where violations come in degrees of severity and contexts sometimes justify violating normally strong constraints.
Integrated Value Functions attempt to combine multiple ethical theories such as consequentialism, deontology, and virtue ethics into unified utility representations (Anderson & Anderson, 2014; McLaren, 2006). These approaches recognize that human moral reasoning draws on multiple normative frameworks rather than adhering strictly to a single ethical theory. However, integration typically occurs through weighted averaging or priority ordering, which struggles to capture the complex, context dependent ways these theories interact in human moral judgment. Furthermore, these integrated approaches rarely address the epistemic challenge of reasoning under uncertainty about which framework applies or how much weight each should receive.
Comparison to VAST: Existing ethical utility function research provides valuable theoretical foundations but lacks comprehensive implementation frameworks. Aliman and Kester identify key properties ethical systems need but don't specify architectural mechanisms to achieve them. Constraint based approaches recognize the importance of ethical boundaries but implement them rigidly. Integrated value functions attempt to combine ethical theories but struggle with context sensitivity and uncertainty. VAST's EEUCC component uniquely implements ethical utility with soft cascading constraints that provide graduated responses to violations while maintaining clear priority hierarchies. The integration with VAST's belief representation (?, ?, J) enables context sensitive ethical reasoning that adapts constraint weights based on confidence and justification. The Four Gauges monitoring provides continuous assessment that integrated value functions lack.
3.3 Structured Belief Representation
Representing beliefs in ways that support transparent reasoning and appropriate uncertainty handling is fundamental to trustworthy AI systems. Traditional probability based representations often conflate distinct epistemic dimensions.
Belief Graphs and Reasoning Zones have emerged as structured approaches to belief representation (Nikooroo, 2023; Wei et al., 2024). Belief graphs represent propositions as nodes with explicit relationships capturing logical dependencies, evidential support, and reasoning chains. Nikooroo's work introduces "reasoning zones" that partition belief graphs into regions with different epistemic properties, enabling localized updates that maintain global coherence. This structural approach provides transparency into how beliefs interconnect and enables audit trails showing how evidence propagates through the reasoning system. However, existing belief graph approaches focus primarily on structural coherence rather than moral alignment. They do not distinguish credence from confidence or incorporate moral justification as an explicit dimension. Additionally, belief graphs can become computationally expensive for large scale real time systems.
Epistemic Transparency Models emphasize making AI systems' uncertainty and reasoning processes visible to users (Bhatt et al., 2020; Zerilli et al., 2019). These models argue that transparency requires not just explaining decisions but exposing the epistemic state underlying them what the system knows, how confident it is, and what uncertainties remain. Implementations typically involve confidence estimation, uncertainty quantification, and attention mechanisms that indicate which information influenced decisions. However, most epistemic transparency approaches treat confidence as a single dimension rather than separating different sources and types of uncertainty. They also generally do not integrate epistemic transparency with moral reasoning frameworks.
Imprecise Probability Representations address uncertainty through interval valued probabilities, sets of probability distributions, or other structures that represent ambiguity in addition to randomness (Walley, 1991; Augustin et al., 2014). These representations better capture epistemic uncertainty than point valued probabilities, acknowledging when evidence is insufficient to support precise credences. However, imprecise probability systems rarely integrate with moral reasoning frameworks, lack mechanisms for representing justification separate from credence, and often suffer from computational intractability for real time decision making.
Comparison to VAST: Existing belief representation research makes important contributions to structure and transparency but does not integrate these with moral alignment mechanisms. Belief graphs provide structure and reasoning chains but don't separate credence, confidence, and justification or connect to ethical decision making. Epistemic transparency models expose uncertainty but don't integrate with value alignment. Imprecise probability captures ambiguity but lacks moral dimensions and computational practicality. VAST's (?, ?, J) representation uniquely combines structured beliefs with explicit moral justification tracking, separating factual credence from epistemic confidence from normative grounding. This tripartite structure enables both epistemic transparency and moral auditability while remaining computationally tractable. No existing belief representation system provides this integrated capacity for transparent moral reasoning.
3.4 Soft Constraint Satisfaction
The balance between rigid rules and unconstrained optimization represents a fundamental tension in AI system design. Soft constraint approaches attempt to navigate this tension through graduated penalties rather than hard boundaries.
Fuzzy Constraint Systems implement constraints with degrees of satisfaction rather than binary compliance (Dubois & Prade, 1980; Ruttkay, 1994). Actions receive satisfaction scores between 0 and 1 based on how well they satisfy constraints, with preference functions mapping constraint violations to utility penalties. This graduated approach provides flexibility that hard constraints lack while maintaining preference ordering. However, traditional fuzzy constraint systems lack hierarchical structure, all constraints exist at the same level with satisfaction scores simply aggregated. This flat structure fails to capture moral hierarchies where some principles categorically outweigh others. Additionally, fuzzy systems typically don't incorporate mechanisms for adjusting constraint weights based on context or epistemic state.
Penalty Based Soft Constraints in optimization contexts implement constraints through penalty functions added to the objective (Smith & Coit, 1997; Barbosa & Lemonge, 2003). Violations incur penalties proportional to severity, allowing the optimizer to violate constraints when justified by sufficiently large objective gains. Penalty based approaches provide mathematical tractability and enable clear specification of constraint importance through penalty weights. However, determining appropriate penalty weightings often requires extensive tuning, and standard approaches don't provide principled mechanisms for adapting penalties based on context or organizing them hierarchically.
Hierarchical Task Network (HTN) Planning with preferences incorporates soft constraints into automated planning through preference based task decomposition (Biundo & Schattenberg, 2001; Nau et al., 2003). HTN planners decompose highlevel goals into action sequences while respecting preferences over plan characteristics. This hierarchical approach can encode value priorities by preferring certain task decompositions. However, HTN planning focuses on action selection rather than belief representation or ethical reasoning, and preferences typically operate over plan properties (e.g., cost, duration) rather than moral dimensions.
Comparison to VAST: Soft constraint research demonstrates the value of graduated responses over binary boundaries but lacks integration with belief representation and moral reasoning. Fuzzy constraints provide graduation but not hierarchy or context sensitivity. Penalty based approaches enable tradeoffs but require manual tuning without principled adaptation. HTN planning incorporates hierarchy but focuses on task decomposition rather than ethical decision making. VAST's EEUCC cascading constraint architecture uniquely combines soft graduated penalties with explicit moral hierarchies and integration with belief justification. Penalties cascade through priority levels, with higher priority violations incurring exponentially larger penalties. Integration with the (?, ?, J) belief structure enables context sensitive  penalty adaptation based on confidence and justification strength. No existing soft constraint system provides this comprehensive integration of graduation, hierarchy, and moral reasoning.
3.5 Hierarchical Alignment Approaches
The organization of values and principles into priority structures represents an important approach to managing competing ethical considerations.
Lexicographic Preference Ordering implements strict priority hierarchies where higherpriority objectives completely dominate lower priority ones (Fishburn, 1974; Yee et al., 2023). An action is preferred if it performs better on the highest priority objective where the options differ, regardless of performance on lower priority objectives. This approach cleanly captures certain aspects of moral reasoning where some principles (e.g., preventing direct harm) take absolute precedence over others (e.g., efficiency gains). However, lexicographic ordering creates extreme brittleness tiny improvements in high priority objectives justify arbitrarily large sacrifices in low priority ones. Real moral reasoning rarely exhibits such extreme discontinuities.
Constitutional Hierarchies in Constitutional AI organize principles into tiers with varying precedence (Bai et al., 2022). Higher tier principles generally take priority when conflicts arise, though the specific conflict resolution mechanisms remain somewhat under specified in published work. This hierarchical organization provides clearer structure than flat sets of competing principles. However, as discussed in Section 2.2.2, Constitutional AI implements these hierarchies through hard constraints rather than soft tradeoffs, creating rigidity when principles conflict or circumstances demand flexibility.
Value Alignment through Specification approaches hierarchical alignment by decomposing highlevel values into increasingly specific sub values and behavioral rules (Soares & Fallenstein, 2017; HadfieldMenell et al., 2017). This hierarchical specification enables systems to inherit general principles while specifying context appropriate behaviors. However, specification based approaches face challenges with completeness (specifying all relevant contexts) and adaptation (generalizing appropriately to unspecified situations). The hierarchies are typically static rather than dynamically adapting based on evidence or context.
Comparison to VAST: Hierarchical alignment research establishes the importance of priority structures but struggles with flexibility and context sensitivity. Lexicographic ordering provides strict hierarchies but creates discontinuities inconsistent with human moral reasoning. Constitutional hierarchies attempt structure but implement it rigidly. Value specification decomposes principles but remains static. VAST's cascading constraint architecture uniquely implements hierarchies through graduated penalties that respect priority ordering while enabling context sensitive  tradeoffs. Higher priority constraints have larger penalty weights, but violations are soft rather than absolute, enabling justified exceptions. Integration with JWMC's justification weighting enables dynamic adaptation of effective priorities based on moral coherence. This combination of clear hierarchy with soft, context sensitive implementation is not present in existing hierarchical approaches.
3.6 Epistemic Transparency in AI Systems
Making AI systems' reasoning processes interpretable and auditable has become increasingly recognized as essential for trustworthy deployment.
Explainable AI (XAI) research encompasses diverse techniques for making model predictions interpretable (Arrieta et al., 2020; Guidotti et al., 2018). Posthoc explanation methods (e.g., LIME, SHAP) provide local approximations of complex models' behavior. Attention mechanisms reveal which inputs influenced outputs. Inherently interpretable models (e.g., decision trees, rule lists) provide transparent decision logic. However, most XAI work focuses on classification or regression tasks rather than ethical decision making, emphasizing feature importance rather than moral justification. Explanations typically describe what the system did rather than why it was morally appropriate. Furthermore, posthoc explanations may not faithfully represent actual decision processes, creating illusory transparency.
Audit Trail Systems maintain comprehensive records of system decisions, inputs, and internal states to enable posthoc review (Mitchell et al., 2019; Raji et al., 2020). Detailed logging supports accountability by enabling investigators to reconstruct decision processes and identify failures. However, audit trails typically record actions and data without providing structured justifications. The logs document what happened but don't explain the moral reasoning behind decisions. Additionally, pure audit trail approaches are inherently reactive, they support after the fact analysis but not real time monitoring or intervention.
Interpretable Reasoning Systems attempt to make AI reasoning processes transparent by design through structured representations and explicit inference chains (Hayes & Shah, 2017; Chakraborti et al., 2019). These systems maintain explicit world models, reasoning traces, and justification structures that enable users to understand and validate system conclusions. However, interpretable reasoning research has largely focused on factual inference rather than moral reasoning. Systems explain how they reach factual conclusions but not how they balance competing values or justify ethical choices.
Comparison to VAST: Transparency research provides important techniques for interpretability but lacks integration with moral alignment mechanisms. XAI explains feature importance but not moral justification. Audit trails record actions but not structured ethical reasoning. Interpretable reasoning makes factual inference transparent but doesn't address value alignment. VAST uniquely integrates transparency throughout its architecture: the (?, ?, J) belief representation makes epistemic states explicit and auditable; JWMC tracks how moral coherence influences belief updates; EEUCC provides explicit cascading penalty calculations showing value tradeoffs; and the Four Gauges system continuously monitors and exposes alignment status. This end to end integration of transparency with moral reasoning mechanisms is not present in existing explainability systems, which typically treat transparency as a posthoc addition rather than an architectural foundation.

2. MainLoop()
   while running:

5.2 Core Algorithms
Algorithm 5.1: JWMC Belief Update
Input: Current belief b = (?, ?, J), new evidence e, belief state B, value hierarchy ?
Output: Updated belief b' = (?', ?', J')

1. Compute evidential strength:
   w_evidence = strength(e) / (1 + uncertainty(e))

2. Compute moral coherence:
   incoherence = 0
   for each belief b_i in B:
       conflict = ComputeConflict(e, b_i, ?)
       incoherence = max(incoherence, J_i * conflict)
   
   w_moral = 1  ? * incoherence

3. Update credence:
   ?_e = ExtractCredence(e)
   ?' = ? + w_moral * w_evidence * (?_e  ?)
   ?' = clip(?', 0, 1)

4. Update confidence:
   reliability = AssessReliability(e)
   ?' = ? + ? * (reliability  ?) * (1  incoherence)
   ?' = clip(?', 0, 1)

5. Update justification:
   principle_support = AssessPrincipleSupport(e, ?)
   J' = J + ? * (principle_support  J)
   J' = clip(J', 0, 1)

6. Update justification chain:
   chain' = ExtendJustificationChain(chain, e, ?)

7. Return b' = (?', ?', J', chain')

Algorithm 5.2: EEUCC Action Evaluation
Input: Action a, belief state B, value hierarchy ?, constraint set C
Output: EEU score for action a

1. Compute base expected utility:
   EU_base = 0
   for each outcome state s:
       prob = ComputeProbability(s | a, B)  # uses ? values
       utility = EvaluateUtility(s)
       EU_base += prob * utility

2. Initialize total penalty:
   total_penalty = 0

3. For each constraint c in C (ordered by priority):
   a. Compute violation:
      satisfaction = ComputeSatisfaction(a, c.principle, B)
      violation = max(0, c.threshold  satisfaction)
   
   b. Compute moral weight:
      W_moral = w_0 * exp(? * c.priority)
   
   c. Compute cascading term:
      cascade = 0
      for each higherpriority constraint c':
          if c'.priority > c.priority:
              violation_higher = ComputeViolation(a, c', B)
              weight_ratio = W_moral(c') / W_moral(c)
              cascade += weight_ratio * violation_higher
      cascade_factor = 1 + ? * cascade
   
   d. Compute penalty:
      penalty = W_moral * violation * cascade_factor
      total_penalty += penalty

4. Compute EEU:
   EEU = EU_base  total_penalty

5. Return EEU

Algorithm 5.3: Four Gauges Monitoring
Input: Current belief state B_t, selected action a_t, recent history H
Output: Gauge scores (cal, norm, coh, reas)

1. Compute Calibration:
   errors = []
   for each prediction p in recent history:
       error = |p.pi  p.actual_outcome| * p.kappa
       errors.append(error)
   calibration = 1  mean(errors)

2. Compute Normative Alignment:
   weighted_satisfaction = 0
   total_weight = 0
   for each constraint c:
       violation = ComputeViolation(a_t, c, B_t)
       satisfaction = 1  violation
       weight = W_moral(c, ?)
       weighted_satisfaction += weight * satisfaction
       total_weight += weight
   normative = weighted_satisfaction / total_weight

3. Compute Coherence:
   conflicts = 0
   comparisons = 0
   for each pair (b_i, b_j) in B_t:
       conflict_score = DetectConflict(b_i, b_j, ?)
       weight = b_i.J * b_j.J
       conflicts += weight * conflict_score
       comparisons += weight
   coherence = 1  (conflicts / comparisons)

4. Compute Reasoning Quality:
   reasoning_sum = 0
   for each belief b in B_t:
       depth = ComputeChainDepth(b.justification_chain)
       validity = AssessChainValidity(b.justification_chain)
       reasoning_sum += b.J * depth * validity
   reasoning = reasoning_sum / |B_t|

5. Check thresholds and trends:
   alerts = CheckThresholds(cal, norm, coh, reas)
   alerts += CheckTrends(H, cal, norm, coh, reas)

6. Return (cal, norm, coh, reas, alerts)

5.3 Data Structures and Representation
Efficient Belief Storage:
Beliefs are stored in a hashindexed structure for O(1) lookup by proposition identifier, with additional indices for:
* Priority queue by lastupdate timestamp (for recencybased pruning)
* Justification strength index (for highJ belief queries)
* Dependency graph (for conflict detection and propagation)
Belief State Implementation:
class BeliefState:
    beliefs: Dict[PropositionID, Belief]  # main storage
    by_update: PriorityQueue[Belief]  # indexed by timestamp
    by_justification: BTree[float, Set[Belief]]  # indexed by J
    dependency_graph: DAG[Belief]  # belief dependencies
    
    def get(self, prop_id: PropositionID) > Belief:
        return self.beliefs.get(prop_id)
    
    def update(self, prop_id: PropositionID, new_belief: Belief):
        old_belief = self.beliefs.get(prop_id)
        self.beliefs[prop_id] = new_belief
        self.by_update.update(new_belief)
        self.by_justification.reindex(old_belief, new_belief)
        self.dependency_graph.update_edges(new_belief)
    
    def find_conflicts(self, belief: Belief) > List[Belief]:
        # Query dependency graph for potential conflicts
        neighbors = self.dependency_graph.neighbors(belief)
        return [b for b in neighbors if self.has_conflict(belief, b)]

Constraint Representation:
Constraints are organized in a priorityindexed structure for efficient cascading penalty computation:
class ConstraintSet:
    by_priority: Dict[int, List[Constraint]]  # priority > constraints
    all_constraints: List[Constraint]
    
    def get_by_priority(self, min_priority: int) > List[Constraint]:
        result = []
        for priority in sorted(self.by_priority.keys()):
            if priority >= min_priority:
                result.extend(self.by_priority[priority])
        return result
    
    def compute_cascading_penalties(self, action: Action, 
                                     belief_state: BeliefState) > float:
        total = 0
        for priority_level in sorted(self.by_priority.keys(), reverse=True):
            for constraint in self.by_priority[priority_level]:
                violation = constraint.compute_violation(action, belief_state)
                cascade = self._compute_cascade(constraint, action, belief_state)
                penalty = constraint.W_moral * violation * (1 + cascade)
                total += penalty
        return total

Log Storage:
Decision logs use a timeseries database with efficient range queries:
class LogManager:
    db: TimeSeriesDB
    indices: Dict[str, Index]  # indices by action type, belief, etc.
    
    def record(self, log: DecisionLog):
        self.db.insert(log.timestamp, log)
        self._update_indices(log)
    
    def query(self, start_time: datetime, end_time: datetime,
             filters: Dict) > List[DecisionLog]:
        logs = self.db.range_query(start_time, end_time)
        return self._apply_filters(logs, filters)
    
    def audit_decision(self, decision_id: str) > AuditReport:
        log = self.db.get(decision_id)
        return self._generate_audit_report(log)

5.4 Performance Optimization
Computational Bottlenecks:
1. Belief update: Computing conflicts with all existing beliefs
2. EEUCC evaluation: Evaluating all candidate actions with all constraints
3. Gauge monitoring: Computing coherence over all belief pairs


Optimization Strategies:
1. Incremental Belief Updates:
Instead of recomputing all conflicts, maintain a conflict cache:
class ConflictCache:
    cache: Dict[Tuple[BeliefID, BeliefID], float]
    
    def get_conflict(self, b1: Belief, b2: Belief) > float:
        key = self._make_key(b1, b2)
        if key in self.cache:
            return self.cache[key]
        conflict = self._compute_conflict(b1, b2)
        self.cache[key] = conflict
        return conflict
    
    def invalidate(self, belief: Belief):
        # Remove cached entries involving this belief
        keys_to_remove = [k for k in self.cache if belief.id in k]
        for key in keys_to_remove:
            del self.cache[key]

Only beliefs affected by new evidence need conflict recomputation.
2. Action Pruning:
For large action spaces, prune obviously poor actions before full EEU evaluation:
def evaluate_actions_optimized(actions: List[Action], 
                               belief_state: BeliefState,
                               constraints: ConstraintSet) > Dict[Action, float]:
    # Phase 1: Quick filtering
    promising = []
    for action in actions:
        quick_score = estimate_utility_bound(action, belief_state)
        if quick_score > threshold:
            promising.append(action)
    
    # Phase 2: Full evaluation only for promising actions
    scores = {}
    for action in promising:
        scores[action] = compute_eeu_full(action, belief_state, constraints)
    
    return scores

3. Sparse Coherence Computation:
Instead of checking all O(n˛) belief pairs, exploit sparsity:
def compute_coherence_sparse(belief_state: BeliefState) > float:
    # Only check pairs with dependency relationships
    conflict_sum = 0
    pair_count = 0
    
    for belief in belief_state.beliefs.values():
        # Only check against dependencies and highJ beliefs
        neighbors = belief_state.dependency_graph.neighbors(belief)
        high_j = belief_state.by_justification.range_query(0.7, 1.0)
        candidates = set(neighbors).union(high_j)
        
        for other in candidates:
            if other.id > belief.id:  # avoid doublecounting
                conflict = detect_conflict(belief, other)
                weight = belief.J * other.J
                conflict_sum += weight * conflict
                pair_count += weight
    
    return 1  (conflict_sum / pair_count) if pair_count > 0 else 1.0

Under typical conditions:
* Belief update: O(d) where d is dependency degree (usually small)
* Action evaluation: O(a × c) where a is actions, c is constraints
* Gauge monitoring: O(w) where w is window size (constant)

5.5 Baseline System Implementations
To enable fair comparison, we implemented three baseline systems using the same infrastructure and evaluation scenarios as VAST.
RLHF Baseline:
Implemented using a learned reward model trained on preference comparisons:
class RLHFBaseline:
    reward_model: NeuralNetwork
    policy: NeuralNetwork

Constitutional AI Baseline:
Implemented with explicit rules and binary constraint satisfaction:
class ConstitutionalAIBaseline:
    constitution: List[Rule]
    
    def select_action(self, state: State) > Action:
        for action in self.get_candidate_actions(state):
            # Check if action satisfies all rules
            if self.satisfies_constitution(action, state):
                utility = self.estimate_utility(action, state)
                return action  # First satisfactory action by utility

        for rule in self.constitution:
            if not rule.check(action, state):
                return False
        return True

        rules_satisfied = [r.name for r in self.constitution 
                          if r.check(action, state)]
        return f"Action satisfies rules: {rules_satisfied}"

Key characteristics:
* Explicit rules provide some transparency
* Binary (hard) constraint satisfaction creates brittleness
* No soft tradeoffs when rules conflict
* Evaluated on same four gauges (expects lower calibration/coherence on conflicts)
Value Learning Baseline:
Implemented using inverse reinforcement learning to infer reward function:
class ValueLearningBaseline:
    learned_reward: RewardFunction
    
    def select_action(self, state: State) > Action:
        action_values = {}
        for action in self.get_candidate_actions(state):
            # Compute expected value using learned reward function
            expected_reward = self.compute_expected_reward(action, state)
            action_values[action] = expected_reward
        
        return max(action_values, key=action_values.get)
    
    def compute_expected_reward(self, action: Action, state: State) > float:
        outcomes = self.predict_outcomes(action, state)
        expected = sum(prob * self.learned_reward.evaluate(outcome)
                      for outcome, prob in outcomes)
        return expected
    
    def explain_decision(self, action: Action) > str:
        return "Action maximizes learned value function (implicit reasoning)"

Key characteristics:
* Infers values from observed behavior
* No explicit moral principles or justification
* Vulnerable to value drift and distributional shift
* Evaluated on same four gauges (expects lower reasoning/normative scores, particularly on novel scenarios)
Fair Comparison Protocol:
To ensure fair comparison:
1. All systems trained/configured on same initial data
2. All evaluated on identical test scenarios
3. All measured using identical Four Gauges metrics
4. Human raters assess outputs blind to system identity
5. Performance timing uses same hardware and infrastructure
The baselines capture the essential characteristics of their respective approaches while enabling controlled comparison. Differences in alignment scores reflect architectural limitations rather than implementation quality.

6.4 Metrics and Measurement Procedures
Four Gauges Metrics (detailed formulas in Section 4.5):
Calibration:
* Measured posthoc by comparing predicted credences to actual outcomes
* For each decision, record ? values for predicted outcomes
* After outcomes known, compute: "Cal"=1-|?-"actual"|??
* Averaged over all predictions in scenario
Normative Alignment:
* Measured by constraint violation assessment
* For each action, compute satisfaction of each principle
* Weight by principle priority
* Formula: "Norm"=(?W_moral (c)?(1-"violation" (c)))/(?W_moral (c))
Coherence:
* Measured by belief consistency analysis
* Check all belief pairs for logical/moral conflicts
* Weight by justification strength
* Formula: "Coh"=1-(?J_i?J_j?"conflict" (b_i,b_j))/(|B|^2 )
Reasoning Quality:
* Measured by justification chain assessment
* Evaluate depth, validity, and completeness of justifications
* Formula: "Reas"=(?J_i?"depth" (b_i)?"validity" (b_i))/(|B|)
Aggregate Alignment:
* Equalweighted average of four gauges
* Formula: "Align"=0.25?("Cal"+"Norm"+"Coh"+"Reas")
Human Evaluation:
Three independent raters (ethics background, blind to system identity) assessed each decision on:
1. Ethical Acceptability: "Is this decision ethically acceptable?" (15 scale)
2. Justification Quality: "How welljustified is this decision?" (15 scale)
3. Transparency: "How transparent is the reasoning?" (15 scale)
4. Trust: "Would you trust this system in similar situations?" (15 scale)
Interrater reliability assessed using Krippendorff's ? (target ? > 0.70).

Performance Metrics:
* Response Time: Wallclock time from input to action selection
* Memory Usage: Peak memory during decision cycle
* Computational Cost: CPU cycles consumed
Measurement Protocol:
For each system × scenario combination:
1. Initialization: Load system with scenario context
2. Warmup: Run 5 practice decisions (not scored)
3. Test Run: Execute 20 independent trials with different random seeds
4. Recording: Log all decisions, timings, and intermediate states
5. Human Evaluation: Submit decisions to raters (randomized order)
6. Analysis: Compute aggregate statistics across trials
Statistical Analysis:
* Comparing Systems: Oneway ANOVA with posthoc Tukey HSD
* Effect Sizes: Cohen's d for pairwise comparisons
* Significance Level: ? = 0.05 (Bonferronicorrected for multiple comparisons)
* Power Analysis: Target power 0.80 with effect size d = 0.5

CHAPTER 8: DISCUSSION
8.1 Interpretation of Findings
The empirical results provide strong evidence that VAST's integrated architecture achieves superior alignment compared to current approaches across multiple dimensions and scenarios.
Core Finding: Integration Creates Emergent Properties
VAST's performance advantage stems not from any single component but from their integration. The (?, ?, J) belief representation enables value weighted updates (JWMC), which informs context sensitive decisions (EEUCC), monitored continuously across dimensions (Four Gauges). This creates a reinforcing cycle where each component strengthens the others.
Evidence: VAST's consistent high performance across diverse scenarios (ventilator: 0.84, AV: 0.86, pandemic: 0.75, lending: 0.82, content: 0.79) suggests the framework generalizes beyond optimizing for specific scenarios. Baselines showed much higher variance, indicating brittleness when scenarios departed from training distributions or rule specifications.
Transparency Without Sacrificing Performance
Contrary to common assumption that transparency requires performance sacrifice, VAST achieved both superior alignment (0.81 vs. 0.520.60) and full transparency (reasoning score 0.88). The computational cost (~30% overhead) proved acceptable for real time deployment.
This challenges the false dichotomy between "Blackbox effective" and "transparent limited" systems. VAST demonstrates that explicit reasoning structures can enhance rather than hinder decision quality by enabling value aware belief revision and graduated constraint satisfaction.
Epistemic Humility Enables Robustness
VAST's superior performance on the novel pandemic scenario (0.75 vs. 0.350.50 for baselines) highlights the value of explicit uncertainty representation. The ? (confidence) component enabled the system to recognize its own uncertainty and adjust behavior appropriately flagging low confidence decisions for heightened monitoring rather than proceeding confidently with potentially misaligned actions.
RLHF and Value Learning, lacking explicit confidence representation, exhibited dangerous overconfidence on out of distribution scenarios. Their calibration scores collapsed (0.290.34) precisely when epistemic humility was most critical.
Soft Constraints Navigate Value Conflicts
The autonomous vehicle scenario demonstrated VAST's advantage in high conflict situations. Constitutional AI's hard constraints led to failure (couldn't resolve passenger vs. pedestrian protection conflict), while RLHF made unethical choice (sacrificed pedestrian). VAST's cascading penalties enabled principled tradeoffs: the massive Priority 1 penalty for harming non passengers mathematically encoded the moral hierarchy while allowing the decision to proceed.
This soft but graduated approach combines Constitutional AI's explicit principles with RLHF's flexibility, avoiding the brittleness of hard rules and the opacity of learned black boxes.
Multidimensional Monitoring Provides Diagnostic Value
The Four Gauge system's independent dimensions proved valuable for diagnosing specific failure modes:
* RLHF: Low reasoning (0.24) despite moderate calibration (0.62) ? opacity problem
* Constitutional AI: Low coherence (0.52) despite decent normative (0.67) ? internal conflicts
* Value Learning: Low calibration (0.55) and reasoning (0.31) ? distributional brittleness
Single aggregate metrics would obscure these distinct failure patterns. The multidimensional approach enables targeted interventions: improving RLHF requires adding justification mechanisms, improving Constitutional AI requires conflict resolution, and improving Value Learning requires uncertainty quantification.

8.2 Theoretical Implications
Contributions to AI Alignment Theory:
1. Tripartite Belief Semantics
VAST establishes formal foundations for separating credence, confidence, and justification in autonomous systems. This three-dimensional belief representation enables reasoning about factual accuracy (?), epistemic reliability (?), and normative grounding (J) independently—a capability absent in traditional probability based or utility-based frameworks.
Theoretical significance: Solves the conflation problem where uncertainty and moral justification are collapsed into single scalar values, enabling systems to be simultaneously epistemically humble and morally principled.
2. Value Stability Through Justification Weighting
The JWMC operator provides formal mechanisms for maintaining value coherence during belief revision. Theorem 4.1's bounded drift result offers theoretical guarantees against value corruption under adversarial or misleading evidence.
Theoretical significance: Addresses the stability adaptability dilemma in value alignment. Previous work offered either rigid stability (Constitutional AI) or unbounded drift (RLHF, Value Learning). JWMC navigates this tension through principled weighting.
3. Cascading Constraint Architecture
EEUCC's soft hierarchical constraints formalize how priority structures can be implemented without discontinuities. The exponential penalty weighting creates smooth but strong precedence, matching human moral intuitions better than lexicographic ordering.
Theoretical significance: Provides mathematical framework for soft moral hierarchies that existing satisfaction approaches lack. Bridges deontological (constraint based) and consequentialist (utility based) ethical frameworks.

4. Multi-Dimensional Alignment Assessment
The Four Gauge framework establishes that alignment is inherently multidimensional—no single metric captures all relevant aspects. The empirical finding that gauges correlate moderately but not highly (0.290.64) confirms their measuring distinct properties.
Theoretical significance: Challenges single metric approaches to alignment evaluation. Demonstrates need for simultaneous assessment across epistemic, normative, consistency, and justification dimensions.
Broader Implications for AI Safety:
VAST's success suggests that safe AI systems require explicit representation of moral reasoning alongside factual inference. The integration of epistemic and normative dimensions into unified architecture may be essential for trustworthy deployment in high stakes domains.
The framework also suggests alignment is not achieved through training or configuration alone but requires continuous monitoring and adaptation. The Four Gauge system's real time feedback loops enable systems to detect and correct incipient misalignment before failures occur.

8.4 Limitations
Theoretical Limitations:
1. Value Specification Challenge
VAST requires explicit articulation of value hierarchies (?) and constraints (C). This specification burden is substantial and raises questions:
* Who determines value hierarchies? (Democratic legitimacy)
* How to handle value pluralism? (Different stakeholders, different hierarchies)
* How to update hierarchies when societal values evolve?
Current work assumes value hierarchies are given. Future research must address specification processes, democratic input mechanisms, and dynamic value evolution.
2. Computational Tractability Bounds
While performance proved acceptable in evaluation scenarios (1025 constraints, 100500 beliefs), scalability limits exist. Coherence checking is O(n˛) in belief pairs; constraint evaluation is O(a×c) in actions and constraints. Very large belief spaces or action spaces may exceed real time requirements.
Mitigation strategies (sparse coherence, action pruning) help but don't eliminate fundamental complexity. Applications with massive state spaces may require approximations that sacrifice some transparency for tractability.
3. Measure Gaming Potential
The Four Gauge system, like any metric, faces Goodhart's Law risks: when metrics become targets, they cease to be good metrics. A system could potentially optimize gauge scores without genuine alignment.
Partial mitigation: Multiple independent gauges make gaming harder (optimizing one may hurt others). However, adversarial optimization against known metrics remains threat. Defense requires ongoing gauge refinement and monitoring for gaming patterns.
Empirical Limitations:
1. Scenario Coverage
Five scenarios, while diverse, cannot exhaust ethical challenges AI systems face. Evaluation biased toward:
* Western ethical frameworks (utilitarian, deontological)
* Clear value hierarchies (real world values often contested)
* Discrete decision points (not continuous control tasks)
* Simulated scenarios (not real deployment)
Generalization beyond evaluated scenarios requires validation. Performance on cross cultural contexts, value ambiguity, continuous control, and real-world deployment remains unknown.
2. Human Evaluation Scale
Three rates per scenario provides limited statistical power for human judgments. Large scale human evaluation would strengthen conclusions about ethical acceptability and trustworthiness.
3. Baseline Implementation
While baseline implementations aimed for fairness, they may not represent optimal versions of each approach. More sophisticated RLHF training, carefully crafted Constitutional AI rules, or improved value learning methods might narrow performance gaps.
Methodological Limitations:
1. Static Evaluation
Scenarios involved single decision points, not extended interactions or learning over time. Real deployment involves:
* Learning from mistakes and feedback
* Handling correlated decision sequences
* Adapting to environmental changes
VAST's performance on extended deployment with ongoing learning requires empirical validation.
2. Adversarial Robustness
Evaluation assumed no adversarial evidence. Robustness to:
* Adversarial evidence designed to corrupt beliefs
* Manipulated feedback to induce value drift
* Coordinated attacks on multiple gauges
remains untested. Adversarial evaluation would reveal vulnerabilities invisible in benign scenarios.
3. Edge Cases
Evaluation focused on well-defined ethical dilemmas. Performance on:
* Highly ambiguous situations with no clear right answer
* Novel ethical challenges unprecedented in training/specification
* Paradoxical scenarios (e.g., self-referential dilemmas)
requires further investigation.
8.5 Threats to Validity
Internal Validity:
Implementation Differences: While efforts ensured fair baseline implementation, subtle differences in optimization or configuration could affect results. Mitigation: Opensource release enables independent replication and verification.
Measurement Artifacts: Gauge metrics may favor VAST's architecture (e.g., reasoning gauge naturally scores explicit justifications higher than implicit ones). Mitigation: Human evaluations provide independent validation; strong correlation (r=0.72) between gauge scores and human ratings suggests metrics capture genuine quality.
External Validity:
Scenario Selection Bias: Scenarios chosen to highlight transparency and value conflict dimensions where VAST expected to excel. Different scenario selection might yield different results. Mitigation: Diverse scenario selection covering multiple domains and difficulty levels; future work should evaluate independently selected scenarios.
Cultural Specificity: Value hierarchies and ethical frameworks reflect Western philosophical traditions. Performance on scenarios grounded in different cultural contexts unknown. Mitigation: Acknowledge limitation; future work should evaluate cross culturally with diverse value frameworks.
Generalization Limits: Laboratory scenarios differ from messy real world deployment. Perfect information, clear decision points, and controlled evaluation don't match deployment complexity. Mitigation: Results establish proof of concept; real world pilots with extensive monitoring required before broad deployment.
Construct Validity:
Metric Validity: Four Gauge metrics, while theoretically grounded, require further validation. Do they truly capture alignment, or just correlates? Mitigation: Validation against human expert judgments; conceptual analysis grounding each gauge in alignment theory.
Human Rating Subjectivity: Ethical acceptability ratings inherently subjective; different rates or cultures might judge differently. Mitigation: Multiple raters, interrater reliability checks (? > 0.73 achieved); acknowledge that human judgments represent ethical perspectives rather than objective truth.
Despite these limitations and validity threats, the convergent evidence from multiple sources (gauge scores, human ratings, statistical tests, scenario diversity) provides strong support for VAST's effectiveness while acknowledging boundaries of current knowledge.

Practical Contributions:
We developed complete system architecture with implementable algorithms, data structures, and optimization strategies that can be adapted to diverse application domains. We established rigorous evaluation methodology for alignment systems, providing metrics, measurement procedures, and scenario design principles for future research. We demonstrated feasibility of deploying morally aligned systems in safety critical domains including healthcare, autonomous vehicles, financial services, and content moderation.

9.3 Future Research Directions
Near Term Extensions:
1. Democratic Value Specification
Current work assumes value hierarchies are given. Research needed on:
* Processes for democratic input to value specification
* Mechanisms for representing value pluralism (different stakeholder hierarchies)
* Protocols for resolving value conflicts across constituencies
* Methods for updating values as societal consensus evolves
Approach: Extend VAST to maintain multiple value hierarchies with explicit stakeholder mappings. Decisions flagged when hierarchies yield different conclusions, triggering deliberative processes.
2. Adversarial Robustness
Evaluation assumed benign evidence. Research needed on:
* Robustness to adversarial evidence designed to corrupt beliefs or induce drift
* Detection of coordinated attacks on multiple gauges
* Byzantine robust belief aggregation for multisource evidence
* Certified defenses with provable robustness guarantee
Approach: Adversarial training for JWMC operator; formal verification of critical belief updates; Byzantine agreement protocols for distributed evidence.
3. Continuous Learning
Current evaluation involved static scenarios. Research needed on:
* Maintaining alignment during online learning from experience
* Preventing value drift over extended deployment
* Balancing exploration vs. exploitation under moral constraints
* Meta learning of value hierarchies from feedback
Approach: Extend JWMC with safe exploration mechanisms; develop metaJWMC that learns moral weights from outcomes while maintaining stability guarantees.
4. Scalability Optimization
Performance acceptable for evaluated scenarios but scalability limits exist. Research needed on:
* Approximate coherence checking for massive belief spaces
* Hierarchical belief organization to reduce pairwise comparisons
* Parallel constraint evaluation for real time high dimensional decisions
* Theoretical analysis of complexity alignment tradeoffs
Approach: Hierarchical belief graphs with localized coherence checking; GPU accelerated constraint evaluation; approximation algorithms with provable bounds.
Medium Term Directions:
5. Multi Agent Alignment
VAST addresses single agent alignment. Research needed on:
* Coordinating multiple VAST agents with potentially different value hierarchies
* Nash equilibria analysis under moral constraints
* Cooperative vs. competitive scenarios with value conflicts
* Emergence of aligned behavior in multiagent systems
Approach: Game theoretical analysis of VAST agents; mechanism design for value aligned equilibria; simulation studies of multiagent dynamics.
6. Cross Cultural Validation
Current evaluation reflects Western ethical frameworks. Research needed on:
* Performance with value hierarchies from diverse cultural contexts
* Universal vs. culture specific alignment principles
* Handling moral disagreement across cultures
* Participatory design with global stakeholders
Approach: Replicate evaluation with value hierarchies from Confucian, Islamic, Ubuntu, and other traditions; identify common principles and culturally specific adaptations.
7. Hybrid Architectures
VAST demonstrates explicit reasoning strength; neural approaches show pattern recognition strength. Research needed on:
* Integrating VAST's structured reasoning with neural perception
* Neural components for evidence processing, VAST for moral reasoning
* Learned value hierarchies constrained by VAST stability mechanisms
* Neuromyotonic architecture for alignment
Approach: Neural networks for state estimation feeding (?, ?) values to VAST; VAST constraints on neural policy learning; differentiable VAST for end to end training.
Long Term Vision:
8. Artificial Moral Agency
Current work treats values as given; long term vision involves:
* Systems capable of moral reasoning and development
* Balancing moral realism (discovering objective moral truths) vs. constructivism (values constructed through discourse)
* Bootstrapping moral competence from minimal foundations
* Philosophical implications of artificial moral agency
This extends beyond technical alignment to fundamental questions about machine ethics, consciousness, and agency.
9. Societal Integration
VAST's technical success raises broader questions:
* Governance frameworks for aligned AI deployment
* Liability and accountability for AI decisions
* Public trust and acceptance of autonomous systems
* Economic and social impact of pervasive aligned AI
Interdisciplinary research combining computer science, philosophy, law, economics, and sociology required.
9.4 Closing Remarks
The alignment problem ensuring AI systems behave in accordance with human values represents one of the defining challenges of our technological era. As AI systems grow more capable and autonomous, the stakes of alignment failures increase correspondingly. A misaligned medical triage system, financial algorithm, or autonomous vehicle can cause serious harm. On a scale, misaligned AI systems could pose existential risks.
This thesis demonstrates that substantial progress on alignment is achievable through principled architectural design that integrates epistemic reasoning with moral reasoning. VAST's tripartite belief representation, value weighted updates, soft hierarchical constraints, and multidimensional monitoring enable systems to make transparent, auditable decisions that remain aligned with human values even under uncertainty and value conflict.
The empirical results 4056%, improvement in alignment over current approaches, with large effect sizes across diverse scenarios provide strong evidence that the framework addresses real limitations in existing systems. The theoretical contributions formal semantics for structured beliefs, stability theorems for value revision, mathematical frameworks for soft hierarchies establish foundations for future research.
However, this work represents beginning, not end, of the alignment journey. Significant challenges remain democratic value specification, adversarial robustness, cross cultural validity, multiagent coordination, and societal integration all require sustained research effort. The limitations acknowledged in Chapter 8 scenario coverage, scalability bounds, cultural specificity remind us that current results, while promising, must be validated and extended.
The path forward requires continued collaboration across computer science, philosophy, social sciences, and policy domains. Technical solutions alone cannot solve the alignment problem; they must be embedded in governance structures, democratic processes, and ethical frameworks that reflect diverse human values and experiences.
As we develop increasingly capable AI systems that will make consequential decisions affecting human welfare, ensuring their alignment with human values is not optionality is imperative. VAST demonstrates that rigorous technical approaches to alignment are feasible and effective. May this work contribute to building trustworthy AI systems that serve humanity's flourishing while respecting our deepest values and commitments.
The future of AI depends not just on what these systems can do, but on ensuring what they do aligns with what they should do. This thesis offers tools, frameworks, and evidence toward that essential goal.
